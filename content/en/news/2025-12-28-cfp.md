## What is a "proposal", exactly?

If this is your first time hearing about Frontend & PHP Conference Hokkaido, you may not be familiar with the word "proposal".

I myself only learned what "proposal" meant after I started getting interested in tech conferences.

### Understanding "proposal"

In English, a "proposal" is — well, a proposal: something you suggest or propose. So what exactly is being proposed?

I won't go too deep into the definition of a conference or a session here, but one of the key offerings of any tech conference is the talks given by speakers. Hearing diverse perspectives from other users, engineers, and companies through these sessions is part of what makes conferences exciting.

Sessions come in many shapes — keynotes, lightning talks, panel discussions, and more — and a "proposal" is a suggestion of _what you'd like to talk about_.

Proposal = a suggestion of talk content

So things like "I tried this and learned X — here's what happened" or "I built this new library and want to share it" are exactly what a proposal looks like.

The phrase "CfP" (or "I'm submitting to the CfP") is used in a similar way. CfP stands for Call for Proposals, and it refers to the call/recruitment itself.

### How should I write a proposal?

We've covered "what is a proposal" so far. If you've thought "alright, I want to write one!" while reading, you're probably also thinking…

### What do I even write?

For Frontend & PHP Conference Hokkaido 2026, the main fields you fill in are these two:

- Talk title (up to 70 characters)
- Talk summary (up to 400 characters)

Pretty simple, right…? That said, "simple" can also be hard to fill in. Below are some thoughts — not as the chair, but just as one organizing-team member — on what tends to land well and what styles of writing are perfectly fine.

### Tiny touches that help

- The summary matters more than the title!
  - A catchy title is great, but during selection we focus on the feelings and energy expressed in the summary 👀 Don't get stuck on the title — start by writing the summary.

- Your unique experience
  - You might think "no one wants to hear my story" — but in practice, very few people are doing the exact same thing as you. Different environments, different paths, different domains all lead to different experiences. Please find what's uniquely _yours_ and put that into the summary!

- Try writing "what's in it for the listener"
  - Following the engineer-favorite pattern of "with X, you get Y", if you can say what attendees will gain or learn from your talk, write that in. It really helps.

- The "this is also OK" series
  - The work you're proposing doesn't have to be finished today
    - It does need to be done by the day of the talk — but writing about something you're currently working on and expect to finish in time is totally fine.
  - It doesn't have to be production work
    - This is especially true on the web frontend side: it doesn't have to be from your day job. DIY-style projects, small JavaScript hacks — all welcome.
  - It doesn't have to be a web app
    - Topics outside of typical web apps and dev systems are also welcome.
  - It can be a topic you've already given (or plan to give) elsewhere
    - Not every attendee has heard your talk before! If you think it's a great topic, please give it in many places.
    - Even more so: a great talk is great no matter how many times you hear it (that's what archives are for).
  - It doesn't have to be the latest topic
  - A talk that revisits something from this point in time, half a year later in June, is totally fine.

### Better ways to write a proposal

Look at other people's proposals!

Many conferences run CfPs and receive many submissions. Past proposals are publicly viewable for many fortee-based events, including Frontend Conference Hokkaido 2025 and PHP Conference Hokkaido 2024.

"This phrasing is so clear", "I want to borrow this opening", "I can't go that deep, but I could do it from this angle"…

Definitely use them as references!
